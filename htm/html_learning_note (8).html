3.2 Text Formatting Elements

This chapter covers inline-level text formatting elements used for emphasis, semantics, readability, and meaning preservation.

Bold (<strong>, <b>)

Executive Summary

<strong> represents semantic importance, while <b> only makes text bold visually.

Syntax & Examples 
<p>This is <strong>important</strong> text.</p>
<p>This is <b>visually bold</b> text.</p>

Italic (<em>, <i>)

Executive Summary

<em> adds emphasis that affects meaning, while <i> is purely stylistic.

Syntax & Examples 
<p>Please <em>read carefully</em>.</p>
<p>Foreign words like <i>déjà vu</i> use italics.</p>

Underline (<u>)

Executive Summary

<u> underlines text but has no semantic meaning. Use carefully to avoid confusion with links.

Syntax & Example 
<p>This is <u>underlined</u> text.</p>

Strikethrough (<s>, <del>)

Executive Summary

<s> means "no longer relevant". <del> indicates removed content.

Syntax & Examples 
<p>Old price: <s>$100</s></p>
<p>Updated: <del>This part was removed.</del></p>

Subscript & Superscript (<sub>, <sup>)

Executive Summary

Useful in math, chemical formulas, and footnotes.

Syntax & Examples 
<p>H<sub>2</sub>O is water.</p>
<p>Area = πr<sup>2</sup></p>

Small Text (<small>)

Executive Summary

<small> is used for disclaimers, fine print, or side notes.

Syntax & Example 
<p>Terms apply. <small>Read conditions carefully.</small></p>

Mark / Highlight (<mark>)

Executive Summary

<mark> visually highlights text with a yellow background.

Syntax & Example 
<p>Please <mark>remember this</mark> point.</p>
